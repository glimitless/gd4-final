Renewable Energy Sources

Renewable energy sources are naturally replenishing but flow-limited. They are virtually inexhaustible in duration but limited in the amount of energy that is available per unit of time.

Environmental Impact: Positive - These sources have minimal environmental impact and help reduce greenhouse gas emissions.

Non-Renewable Energy Sources

Non-renewable energy sources come from finite resources that will eventually dwindle, becoming too expensive or environmentally damaging to retrieve.

Environmental Impact: Negative - These sources contribute significantly to greenhouse gas emissions and climate change.

Solar Energy

Solar energy harnesses the sun's power through photovoltaic panels or concentrated solar power systems.

How it works:

  • Photovoltaic cells convert sunlight directly into electricity
  • Solar thermal collectors heat water or other fluids
  • Concentrated solar power uses mirrors to focus sunlight

Environmental Benefits:

  • Zero emissions during operation
  • No air or water pollution
  • Minimal maintenance required
  • Silent operation

Wind Energy

Wind energy converts the kinetic energy of moving air into electricity using wind turbines.

How it works:

  • Wind turns large turbine blades
  • Blades drive a generator through a gearbox
  • Generator converts mechanical energy to electricity

Environmental Benefits:

  • Zero emissions during operation
  • Small land footprint
  • Compatible with agricultural use
  • No water pollution

Hydroelectric Power

Hydroelectric power harnesses the energy of falling or flowing water to generate electricity.

How it works:

  • Water flows through turbines
  • Turbines drive generators
  • Can store energy by pumping water uphill

Environmental Benefits:

  • Zero direct emissions
  • Long facility lifespan
  • Provides flood control
  • Creates recreational areas

Biomass Energy

Biomass energy comes from burning organic materials like wood, crops, and waste.

How it works:

  • Organic matter is burned or converted to fuel
  • Heat drives steam turbines
  • Can produce liquid biofuels

Environmental Benefits:

  • Carbon neutral when sustainably managed
  • Reduces waste in landfills
  • Can use agricultural byproducts
  • Supports local agriculture

Geothermal Energy

Geothermal energy taps into the Earth's internal heat for power generation and direct use.

How it works:

  • Wells access underground heat
  • Steam drives turbines
  • Can provide direct heating

Environmental Benefits:

  • Very low emissions
  • Small land footprint
  • Constant power output
  • Long facility lifespan

Coal Power

Coal power burns fossil fuels to generate electricity, but is a major source of pollution.

How it works:

  • Coal is burned to heat water
  • Steam drives turbines
  • Requires constant fuel input

Environmental Concerns:

  • Major source of CO2 emissions
  • Air and water pollution
  • Mining damages landscapes
  • Ash disposal issues

Natural Gas

Natural gas is cleaner than coal but still contributes to climate change.

How it works:

  • Gas is burned in turbines
  • Combined cycle increases efficiency
  • Requires pipeline infrastructure

Environmental Concerns:

  • Significant CO2 emissions
  • Methane leakage
  • Fracking environmental impacts
  • Pipeline risks

Nuclear Power

Nuclear power uses fission to generate heat for electricity, with no direct CO2 emissions but other environmental concerns.

How it works:

  • Nuclear fission generates heat
  • Steam drives turbines
  • Requires careful safety measures

Environmental Concerns:

  • Radioactive waste storage
  • Water usage for cooling
  • Mining uranium impacts
  • Risk of accidents

Petroleum Power

Petroleum-based power plants burn oil products to generate electricity, contributing to pollution and climate change.

How it works:

  • Oil is burned to create steam
  • Steam drives turbines
  • Requires constant fuel supply

Environmental Concerns:

  • High CO2 emissions
  • Air pollution
  • Oil spill risks
  • Resource depletion
